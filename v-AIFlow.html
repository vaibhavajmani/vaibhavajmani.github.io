Closing the gap between sales and support

v-AIFlow was losing customers, and the exit surveys kept saying the same thing: support did not know who they were talking to. Sales ran on one system, support ran on another, and the two never spoke. This case study walks through the problem, the options considered, and a phased plan to fix it.

v-AIFlow is a fictional company. The problem and the numbers are invented to make the analysis concrete, not drawn from any employer's systems.

The problem

Two teams, two systems, one customer who noticed.

v-AIFlow is a B2B project management tool. Sales runs on Salesforce. Support runs on Zendesk. Neither system knows the other exists, and that gap shows up in the work every day.

  • Support agents cannot see a customer's tier, contract value, or renewal date.
  • Sales reps cannot see open tickets when preparing for a renewal or an upsell.
  • Customer records are entered twice, so the two systems slowly drift apart.
  • An enterprise account waits in the same queue as a free-tier trial.

Churn rose 12 percent over two quarters. Exit surveys pointed at two things: slow support, and support that did not know who the customer was. The second one is the fixable one.

Five groups feel this problem, and they do not feel it the same way.

Customer SuccessEnd userOne view of account health across both tools
EngineeringImplementerClear requirements and a bounded piece of work

In scope

  • Two-way sync between Salesforce and Zendesk
  • Account data: tier, annual contract value, renewal date, assigned CSM
  • Ticket history surfaced on the Salesforce account record
  • Ticket priority set automatically from account tier

Out of scope

  • Replacing either CRM
  • Billing system changes
  • Mobile application changes

The out-of-scope list matters as much as the in-scope one. Early conversations kept drifting toward replacing Zendesk, which is a much larger project that does not solve this problem any faster.

User stories

Support agent

I want to see a customer's tier and renewal date when I open a ticket, so I can judge how to respond before I start typing.

Accepted when: tier, contract value, and renewal date render on ticket open.

Sales rep

I want recent tickets on the account record, so I can raise a problem in the renewal call before the customer does.

Accepted when: open tickets and last five resolved appear on the account.

Customer Success

I want to be assigned automatically to tickets on my accounts, so I hear about trouble without having to watch for it.

Accepted when: CSM field populates within one sync cycle of ticket creation.

Future state

The same ticket, once the two systems talk.

Customer submits a ticket in Zendesk | Ticket is tagged with tier, contract value, and CSM from Salesforce | Enterprise tickets escalate to urgent automatically | Agent resolves it with the full picture | Ticket and resolution sync back to the account record | Sales rep sees the history before the renewal call

Nothing in the agent's workflow changes. They open a ticket the same way they always have. The difference is what is already on the screen when they get there.

Options considered

Three ways to connect two systems, and the reason for picking the smallest one.

Native connector

The built-in Salesforce and Zendesk integration. It covers every high-priority requirement out of the box, needs no new infrastructure, and can be live in weeks rather than quarters.

Trade-off: limited customisation, no room for unusual workflows.

Integration platform

Sync flows built in a tool like Workato. More flexible, and it would handle the renewal-risk flag more elegantly than the native connector does.

Trade-off: subscription cost plus someone who owns the flows.

Custom build

Engineering writes the integration directly against both APIs. Full control over every field and every edge case.

Trade-off: months of build, and a service the team owns forever.

Recommendation: start with the native connector. It meets every high-priority requirement, and it is reversible. Revisit the integration platform only when a real workflow proves the connector cannot do it. The custom build solves a problem v-AIFlow does not have yet.

Rollout

1Requirements sign-off with Sales, Support, and Engineering2 weeks

The pilot phase exists to catch the thing nobody wrote down. Two weeks with one team is cheap. Discovering a broken field mapping after a company-wide rollout is not.

How success is measured

Four numbers, checked at 90 days.

MeasureTodayTarget
First response time4.2 hoursUnder 2 hours
Agent time spent hunting for contextAround 2 hours per weekNear zero
Renewal churn12 percentUnder 7 percent
Enterprise escalation accuracyManual and inconsistentAutomatic on every ticket

Churn is the number the sponsor cares about, but it moves slowly and has many causes. First response time and escalation accuracy move within weeks and are attributable to this change, which makes them the honest early signal.
